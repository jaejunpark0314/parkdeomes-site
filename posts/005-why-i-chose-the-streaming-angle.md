# Why I Chose the Streaming Angle

---

## I.

Before there was a game, there was a chat window.

I was watching a Korean stream. I don't remember whose. The chat was doing what Korean chats do. Donations stacking. 별풍선 popping. Someone typing 극딜 because the streamer had just made an objectively terrible decision and the audience was making sure he knew it. The streamer laughing, deflecting, making it worse. The chat accelerating.

I watched that loop for a while and thought: this is already a game.

Not metaphorically. Structurally. There's a player and an audience. The audience has opinions and the power to express them in real time. The player is performing under observation. The tension is mechanical. The stakes are emotional. Every decision the player makes is being evaluated simultaneously by hundreds of people who disagree with each other about what the right call was.

I wasn't thinking about deckbuilders yet. I was just watching a chat window.

---

## II.

The genre question came later. Once I knew the emotional core, a streamer fighting for survival under live audience scrutiny, I needed a system that could hold it. Turn-based deckbuilding fit for a reason that had nothing to do with *Slay the Spire* specifically.

Deckbuilders are games about decisions made under constraint. Each turn, you have a hand. You have resources. You have a clock. You make choices with incomplete information and live with the results. That's already the structure of a stream. The audience is the constraint you can't fully control. Your deck is what you've built your channel to be. Every combat is a broadcast.

But there was a problem. And the problem took months to name.

Korean streaming culture doesn't translate into mechanics by describing it. You can't just put a donation bar in the corner and call it authentic. The thing that makes watching Korean streams feel specific, the familial pressure, the way viewers feel personally implicated in whether the streamer succeeds or fails, the performative suffering when things go wrong, that's not a UI element. It's a feeling. And feelings are hard to make into numbers.

---

## III.

Darkest Dungeon has a stress system. It's one of the most discussed mechanics in the genre. It works by punishing you not just with damage but with psychological deterioration. Your characters break down in ways that compound and cascade. The stress is the penalty.

I looked at that system for a long time. Then I tried to figure out why it wasn't what I wanted.

The answer took a while. In DD, stress is something that happens to you. It accumulates. It punishes. It makes things worse. There's no upside. It's a resource drain dressed up as atmosphere.

What I wanted was different. I wanted pressure that felt like *being watched*. Not punished. Watched.

The distinction matters. When you're performing in front of an audience, in Korean streaming culture especially, where the relationship between streamer and viewers has this quasi-familial weight, you're not being punished by their presence. You're being charged by it. The 별풍선 going off isn't a threat. It's a dare. It's a hundred people saying: *we believe you can do something with this. Don't waste it.*

That's what Broadcast Pressure is supposed to be. It builds as you play. It doesn't just punish. It amplifies. Your cards hit harder. The enemies get sharper. The room gets louder. The question isn't whether to let it build. It's whether you can handle what happens when it does.

---

## IV.

The hardest part of designing that system wasn't the numbers. It was the vocabulary.

In Korean streaming, everything has a name. Fans have titles. Streamers have catchphrases. The chat has its own grammar. I spent real time reading through how the communities around specific streamers talk, how 고정 멤버 dynamics work, how donation culture creates visible status hierarchies, how the language of pressure and encouragement is inseparable from the meme layer that carries it.

That research exists in the game now. The viewer count. The chat window filling with reactions. The specific terminology that felt right in Korean in a way it doesn't in English. The moment you play a card and the chat explodes: not randomly, but because you did something the audience recognized.

When I started writing the chat pool for JJ Park's: Subscriber Zero™, I kept asking: does this feel like something an actual Korean streaming chat would say? Not a translated version. Not an approximation. The thing itself.

Some of that is right. Some of it I'll only know once real players are in the game.

---

## V.

Here's the honest version of the ending.

I don't know yet if the streaming angle was the right choice. I made the choice before I built the game. The emotional core came first. The chat window, the pressure, the performance. Everything else followed. The genre, the systems, the three playable characters, the zone structure, all of it was built around a feeling I had watching someone get 극딜'd at 2am.

That might be exactly the right way to make a game. Start with the feeling that won't leave you alone. Build toward it.

Or it might be the kind of move that makes total sense to the person making it and requires significant explaining to everyone else.

I'll know more after the first time someone who's never watched a Korean stream plays this game and tells me what they felt. Until then, the chat window is still open. The pressure is still building.

I'm still watching to see what happens.
