# The Wrong Question

There is a specific kind of exhaustion that comes from being very good at the wrong thing.

I know this because I spent the better part of a year applying for jobs in the game industry. Not entry-level jobs. Senior roles at companies I had shipped titles with, in a field I had worked in for years. I knew how to read a TAM analysis. I knew what LTV meant before most people in the room had heard the acronym. I had worked on AAA franchises. I had sat in the meetings where the numbers were made.

Sixty-one interviews. I counted.

I made it to final rounds more times than I can remember. And then, again and again, nothing. No offer. No explanation that quite made sense.

Then one afternoon, my closest friend sent me a text. He is the godfather of my daughter. He does not waste words.

*Try vibe coding. Make a game with AI.*

I almost ignored it.

---

Here is what I thought I knew about making games.

I had spent years on the other side of the table, the marketing side, watching games get built and launched and loved or forgotten. I understood the market. I understood players. I understood what a good pitch deck looked like and what a bad one smelled like.

So when I seriously considered making a game for the first time, I did what any experienced professional would do. I started analyzing.

What genre is growing? What price point converts? What kind of art style travels well internationally? What does the Steam algorithm reward right now?

I spent weeks on this. I filled documents with competitive analysis. I treated it like a product launch I was being paid to run.

And I produced absolutely nothing.

The problem was not the analysis. The analysis was correct. The problem was that I was asking a question that had no personal answer. "What game should I make" is a market research question. It has a hundred defensible answers and none of them are yours.

I had spent so long thinking like a marketer that I had forgotten how to think like someone who makes things.

---

This is not a unique failure mode. It is, in fact, the most common trap for anyone who comes to creative work through the business side of creative industries.

Eric Barone, who made Stardew Valley alone over four and a half years, was not asking what farming sim the market needed. He was trying to make the game he wished existed. Lucas Pope, who built Return of the Obra Dinn, started with a visual problem he found personally fascinating and followed it for four years until it became a game. Toby Fox made Undertale because he was already writing the music in his head.

None of them started with a spreadsheet.

The question is not "what should I make." The question is "what do I already have, and what can I do with it right now." Those two questions feel similar. They are not. One points outward, toward the market. The other points inward, toward the specific person sitting at the desk.

I had been pointing the wrong direction for months.

---

My friend's text arrived on a Wednesday. By Friday, I had Godot installed.

What followed was, without exaggeration, the most genuinely fun I have had in front of a computer since I was seventeen years old in Australia, playing Civilization for the first time and feeling like the ceiling had just been removed from what games could be.

Vibe coding with AI tools is genuinely difficult to explain to someone who has not tried it. The closest I can get is this: it is like having a conversation with a genie who has read every technical manual ever written and has infinite patience for your follow-up questions. You describe what you want. The genie produces it. You say "actually, more like this." The genie adjusts.

I had ideas I had been carrying around for years, things I thought would require a team and a budget and a production timeline. One by one, they started becoming real. Not perfectly. Not instantly. But real.

In the first week, I had a prototype. In that same week, I had a trailer.

I am the kind of person who prefers to be asleep before ten o'clock. During those first ten days, I was not asleep before ten o'clock.

---

The game itself came from two things I already loved.

The first was the deckbuilding roguelike genre. Slay the Spire had rewired something in my brain. The clarity of it, the way every run teaches you something the last run did not. I had logged more hours than I will publicly admit.

The second came from a late-night stream I happened to catch. A group of Korean VTubers was doing something unusual. A forty-person raid. World of Warcraft, forty-man content. All forty of them had their VRChat avatars on screen while playing, little animated faces reacting in real time as raid calls went out and bosses were pulled. It was funny. But that was not the part that stuck.

I watched that and thought: that is a game.

Not the spectacle of it. The psychology of it. The pressure of performing for an audience while genuinely trying to survive. The way the viewer count is both the measure of your success and the thing that makes you anxious. The way a live audience changes every decision you make.

I wrote a game design document that night. It kept growing. It is now twelve versions deep.

I did not ask whether this concept was marketable. I did not run a competitive analysis. I combined the two things that made me want to stay up past ten o'clock, and I started building.

---

I will be honest with you, because this blog will not be worth reading if I am not.

This game is, at its core, a portfolio. I am a marketing professional who has never shipped a line of production code. I am trying to change that fact. If one person buys this game at launch, I will have accomplished something real: a person I have never met decided that what I made was worth money to them.

I spent years helping other people reach that moment. I analyzed their audiences, shaped their messaging, tracked their conversion rates. I knew what success looked like from the outside.

Now I want to know what it feels like from the inside.

That Tuesday text from my friend is still on my phone. I have not replied with anything clever. There is nothing clever to say.

He was right. I was asking the wrong question.

The right question, it turned out, was the one I stopped being afraid to answer.
